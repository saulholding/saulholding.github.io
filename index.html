Predicting the 2020 US Elections

I helped manage and organize two teams in building robust election prediction models. Using demographic, education, economic, and polling data, we used a random forest, a support vector machine, and an XGBoost to predict US presidential elections. I further utilized a neural network to predict the number of seats won by each party in the House of Representatives. The first team published a website of our work along with a GitHub repository (work done in R). The second team published our results in a jupyter notebook (work done in python) which can be found on this GitHub repository. Each group predicted a Biden victory, and the neural network successfully predicted that the Democrats would take over the House.

October 2020 - December 2020

Fetal/Childhood Development and Climate Change

Award: Received First Class Honours. The paper was commended by members of LSE's faculty.

Using US census data from 1920 to 1940, this paper implements a difference-and-difference method to assess the impact of the drought and famine of the Dust Bowl on fetal and childhood development. The paper finds that this natural disaster caused a reduction in adult productivity and an increase in obesity-related health outcomes for at least 2 generations after the event. While further research needs to be conducted, the paper argues for the external validity of these findings. The paper highlights that as the impacts of climate change fall disproportionately on underdeveloped nations, and as the impacts of climate disruption are felt for generations, climate change will likely further exacerbate global inequities.
